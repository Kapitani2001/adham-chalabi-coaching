We've lost the kind of narrative that once helped people endure suffering.

When a flood wiped out a village, or when a plague swept through a city, people didn't see it as meaningless chaos; they believed it was a trial from the gods, a test of faith, or even a necessary cleansing before renewal. This belief didn't erase the pain, but it gave them a way to bear it, to see their suffering as part of a larger story.

Today, we no longer have that shared frame of meaning. Disasters just feel like meaningless cruelty, and that makes them harder to survive emotionally.

Mircea Eliade referred to this as the "terror of history," the despair that arises when we lose any deeper meaning behind what happens to us, because we can no longer perceive tragedy as anything but meaningless chaos; it becomes psychologically unbearable. And Nietzsche would have agreed. He argued that when we lack a "why," suffering becomes intolerable.

This void of meaning is exactly what cult leaders and manipulators prey on. They bombard people with fear, warnings of collapse, end-of-the-world narratives, then offer themselves as the only source of truth or salvation. When people are starved for meaning, they will cling to anything that promises to make sense of their pain.

Nietzsche's response to this is not to go backward, not to resurrect old gods or outdated stories, but to create new values, values that affirm life rather than deny it.

We need to build our own frameworks of meaning, not because the world is safe or fair, but because only meaning allows us to face suffering without breaking down. Without it, we are at the mercy of fear, chaos, and whoever claims to have the answers.

The question isn't whether life is hard, because it is, but whether you have built something inside you that gives you the strength to face it.

Meaning is not given; it must be forged.
